\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Heterogeneous robot teams form coalitions to move loads that no single agent can
move alone. Classical coalition and task-allocation models certify such teams by
cardinality or scalar capacity, and treat communication as an exogenous resource
that is either present or absent~\cite{gerkey2004taxonomy,choi2009cbba}. Two
failure modes escape this view.

First, a coalition may satisfy every strategic-stability condition yet be unable
to produce the demanded \emph{wrench}---the coupled force and torque---because a
scalar capacity discards direction, lever arm, and contact
complementarity~\cite{murray1994manipulation}. Two robots with identical nominal
capacity can differ entirely in the force--torque directions they support.

Second, and more subtly, the very spatial arrangement that makes a coalition
wrench-feasible can fragment the communication graph the team needs to
\emph{estimate the aggregates and prices that define its own equilibrium}. When
every robot is committed to lifting, no agent remains to relay, algebraic
connectivity collapses, and the distributed equilibrium-seeking dynamics lose the
consensus channel they depend on~\cite{ye2017nash,gadjov2019passivity}. The
equilibrium destroys the means of its own computation.

We take these two conditions to be jointly, not separately, necessary:
\begin{quote}
\emph{A Nash-stable coalition is not operationally meaningful if it cannot
generate the required wrench, or if its own deployment destroys the network
needed to compute and maintain that equilibrium.}
\end{quote}

\paragraph*{Related work}
Aggregative and potential games give a compact route to distributed equilibria
when payoffs couple only through population aggregates~%
\cite{jensen2010aggregative,monderer1996potential,koshal2016aggregative}, and
generalized Nash / variational equilibria extend this to shared
constraints~\cite{rosen1965,facchinei2010gnep,facchinei2003fvi}. Distributed
Nash-seeking over networks is by now well
understood~\cite{ye2017nash,gadjov2019passivity}. In parallel, connectivity
control maintains algebraic connectivity $\lambda_2$ as a design
objective~\cite{fiedler1973,ghosh2006growing,zavlanos2011connectivity,bullo2009distributed},
and multi-robot task allocation handles heterogeneity and communication
limits~\cite{gerkey2004taxonomy,choi2009cbba,prorok2017diversity}. In each of
these lines, however, communication is modeled as a constraint or resource that
is \emph{given}, and physical force allocation---when present---is decoupled from
the equilibrium concept. None couples a directional wrench certificate to an
\emph{endogenous} network inside a single equilibrium.

\paragraph*{Contributions}
\begin{itemize}
\item We define the \textbf{WISE equilibrium} (Section~\ref{sec:wise}): a
generalized variational equilibrium that is simultaneously strategically stable,
wrench-feasible, and information self-sustaining on the induced graph.
\item We build it from a \textbf{directional wrench tensor} $\Wten$ and an
\textbf{endogenous Laplacian} $\Lap(x)$ that is affine in the decisions, so the
information constraint $\lamtwo(\Lap(x))\ge\sigmastar$ defines a convex
super-level set (Section~\ref{sec:model}).
\item We give an \textbf{exact tensor-aggregative potential} (Theorem~\ref{thm:potential})
and prove \textbf{existence and team-optimality} of WISE
(Theorem~\ref{thm:existence}), yielding two certification prices
(Corollary~\ref{cor:prices}) and a closed-form critical long-range fraction
(Proposition~\ref{prop:nu}).
\item A compact analytical study (Section~\ref{sec:exp}) shows the two constraints
are jointly necessary and that relay/torque roles emerge from heterogeneity alone.
\end{itemize}
